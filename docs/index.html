Growing Up with K-Pop

Written by: Minji Kim & Eunice Lee
With Caitlyn Ralph
Illustrations: Damien Jeon
Design: Jeff Kardos
Code: Michelle Pera-McGhee
(1996–2006)

Early Childhood

eunice

Minji and I first met at a Korean language school in the Michigan suburbs. It was the early 2000s, and every Saturday morning, our parents, drawn to Detroit by the promise of the auto industry, dropped us off to study Korean language and culture. We were the same age, and in a classroom full of kids wearing sparkly Limited Too tops, oversized Pokémon shirts, and Dragon Ball Z tees, we bonded over K-pop.

We’d meet at Minji’s house, where we’d sit cross-legged in the living room and play BoA’s “No. 1” on CD. Later, when YouTube arrived, we’d crowd around her family desktop and watch BoA’s music videos, our eyes glued to the screen. We watched her on repeat - studying the way she moved, the precision of her choreography, and her cathartic vocals. We’d rewind the same eight-count over and over, trying to get it right. Sometimes we did. But most of the time we’d get it wrong, laugh, and hit play again.

BoA
"No. 1"
Seo Taiji & Boys
"난 알아요 (I Know)"
minji

Modern K-pop is often traced back to the 1990s, when Seo Taiji and Boys – regulars at Moon Night, a club frequented by Black American GIs in Seoul – learned and took hip hop and New Jack Swing to Korean audiences. Their sound shook the music scene and sparked a cultural shift. In the years that followed, as the country recovered from years of political and financial crisis, the Korean government began to see pop culture - including music, television, and film - as a strategic export and form of soft power.

Of course, we didn’t know any of that at the time. It wasn’t “K-Pop”–it was just our everyday life. I remember my parents burnt CDs with their favorite Korean songs to bring a part of home with them before immigrating to Detroit. We would try to sing and rap along to Seo Taiji’s 난 알아요 (I Know) in the car, finding comfort in Korean words blasting in our ears as we drove through the Michigan suburbs.

eunice

I remember the crowded church parties, the air thick with the smell of bulgogi and japchae. At a certain point of the night, the adults would bring out a bulky, personal Karaoke machine that someone had carried all the way from Korea and sing songs from the eighties and nineties. Our childhood drummed to this steady beat until 2006, when Minji’s family left for California and I moved to Korea the following year.

(2006–2012)

Growing Up — Our First Fandom

Super Junior
"U"
minji

My new school sat four miles from Koreatown and was predominantly Korean–a stark contrast from the majority White neighborhood I was coming from. I struggled at first to adjust to the new cultural expectations and norms, to catch jokes and conversations that flew over my head before I could make sense of them.

At recess, my classmates would talk about a K-pop group called Super Junior. At first, I only pretended I knew and liked “SuJu” to fit in. But one day after school, a friend pulled up a music video on her computer: Super Junior’s newest title track, U.

minji

I still remember how my ears perked up at the strum of the guitar intro, how my head bopped involuntarily as the 11 members popped and locked perfectly to the beat. I ran home that day and immediately googled how to torrent music so that I could add it to my mp3 player, giddy at the thought of playing it at lunch the next day. I was officially a fan.

eunice

Around that same time, I also discovered Super Junior and felt the peculiar intensity of being obsessed with a boy band for the first time. I bought their CD, Twins, and inside was a fold-out sheet: each member serving face with their names printed underneath. It was implied you were supposed to pick a favorite. We didn’t call it a “bias” back then - that language hadn’t reached us yet. But for several years, mine was Kibum.

During this time, Minji and I were penpals, keeping in touch over email. One day she wrote that she was an ELF (Ever Lasting Fans, the official Super Junior fandom), and I screamed! It was thrilling to realize that we were both caught up in the same thing, in completely different places, and somehow it became another thread holding our friendship together.

BIGBANG
"Lies"
minji

But as the years went by, our tastes started to change. By middle school, we were fangirling over hot new groups like Girls’ Generation, Wonder Girls, and BIGBANG.

SHINee
"Replay"
minji

These legendary acts were capturing the hearts of the Korean general public and taking the Hallyu Wave all across Asia and into immigrant communities around the world. After my family moved back to Michigan, my high school’s Asian Pacific American Club decided to incorporate K-pop into our yearly cultural show performance. I couldn’t believe that I was learning the choreography for SHINee’s “Replay” with my non-Korean friends, performing in front of an auditorium full of non-Korean classmates. While most of my classmates couldn’t point to Korea on a map, my small but tight-knit community of Asian immigrant kids were streaming K-pop videos on YouTube together after school.

eunice

In 2012, Psy’s Gangnam Style became the first YouTube video to hit one billion videos. At its peak, Gangnam Style was generating over 11 million views per day, and YouTube even had to upgrade its view counter system, which had maxed out at 2,147,483,647 views – a limit based on 32-bit integer coding. Suddenly, Psy was everywhere and, for many people around the world, it was their first exposure to K-pop.

I remember watching Psy teach the Gangnam Style dance to Britney Spears on Ellen and getting chills. I didn’t even love the song, but there was something thrilling about watching the world finally catch up to what we already knew. Korean pop wasn’t just “foreign.” It was fun, silly, catchy, and unmissable.

minji

We were living through the “Golden Age of K-Pop” from opposite sides of the world – one in the homeland, the other in the diaspora. Though we were too young to understand the forces driving this wave, the soft power that South Korea was amassing via these cultural exports, we were authentic participants of the buzz and excitement.

(2012–2017)

College Years — The K-Pop Boom

eunice

Until around 2012, K-pop expanded at a pace that felt possible to keep up with. The industry was carefully managed by the labels, and only a few groups debuted each year. From 1992 to 2002, often referred to as K-pop's first generation, just 33 groups debuted.

But the second generation of K-pop (roughly 2003-2011) marked a shift from the industry's earlier restraints. 81 new acts emerged in just over 9 years, and among them were generation-defining groups like Girls' Generation and BIGBANG. These artists pushed K-pop beyond domestic borders and into broader regional consciousness.

eunice

What had once been a carefully gated system became a full-blown explosion in K-pop’s third generation (roughly 2012–2017) - a period that coincided, for Minji and me, with the start of college. In just six years, 327 groups debuted, and the annual numbers rose at a staggering pace: 40 new acts in 2012, 60 by 2014, and an astonishing peak of 86 in 2017.

This surge unfolded alongside the global breakthroughs of groups like BTS and EXO, whose success reframed international popularity from a long-term aspiration into an immediate industry mandate. Agencies large and small rushed to enter the market, chasing viral potential and overseas fandoms.

EXO
"Growl"
eunice

I must have felt this on some level, because the early 2010s when my attachment to K-pop began to fade. By the time I returned to the U.S. for college in 2014, my attention had already started to drift elsewhere - toward Chicago, a city alive with its own rising artists and scenes. Louis the Child had released “It’s Strange” in 2015, and Chance the Rapper had come out with “Coloring Book” in 2016. These albums and other releases formed the soundtrack of my college years.

eunice

But even as my tastes changed, I could tell K-pop was gaining speed. My friends were saving money to go to KCON, a global K-culture festival, and I started to hear Korean songs play in the mall and on the radio. And if it wasn’t K-pop, I noticed Korea’s influence in other ways, like skincare routines, mukbang videos, and K-dramas.

TWICE
"TT"
minji

Of course, with greater visibility came fiercer competition. The market was saturated, and the pressure to stand out grew. Idol groups and agencies had to rethink their strategies – instead of having set promotion cycles based around Korean TV appearances, groups started interacting with fans year round on social media platforms like YouTube and VLive, a Korean streaming service that allowed celebrities to conduct live chat sessions with their fans.

BTS
"DNA"
minji

The surge of K-pop content, paired with a now thriving social media culture, created the perfect conditions for K-pop fandoms to grow beyond continental lines and form lives of their own. My friends started stanning iconic groups like Red Velvet and BLACKPINK, and I found BTS. I dug through their then-discography of over 200 songs and made a (still-active) group chat with fellow ARMY friends. Together, we’d count down the days until the next comeback and undergo the impossible challenge of getting concert tickets, even making plans to travel across the country just to see them on tour. Once again, stanning a K-pop group gave me a community.

(2018–2023)

Young Adulthood & The Pandemic

minji

After graduating college, I moved to New York, where I was once again in a big new city with more access to Korean culture. I spent many weekends at Koreatown pochas (street-style bars with food), where they would play the newest K-pop music videos on the TVs. When 2 members of the girl group (G)I-DLE (now i-dle) participated in the virtual group K/DA for the game League of Legends, and SM powerhouse group Aespa debuted with an AI and multiverse concept, I knew we had entered a new era. While these innovations felt foreign at first, I was intrigued by the new soundscapes and fresh performances these 4th gen groups were bringing to the scene.

minji

Not long after, the world was shut down by a global pandemic. Hundreds of planned K-pop concerts were cancelled, and groups started releasing more online content than ever to continue connecting with fans.

BTS
"Dynamite"
minji

On August 20, 2020, BTS dropped Dynamite - their first English single and the first K-pop song to debut at number 1 on the Billboard Hot 100. My ARMY friends stayed up until midnight for the premiere, texting reactions in real time. We watched ARMY social media band together to break multiple records: most viewers for a YouTube Premiere, most viewed YouTube video in 24 hours, most weeks on the US Hot 100 by a K-pop track, and more.

eunice

This active social media interaction was a common phenomenon among K-pop fandoms during this time. Just 2 months before Dynamite, How You Like That had broken the same YouTube records that Dynamite did. And with the popularization of TikTok around the globe, the newest 4th gen groups were able to grow massive fandoms even without having yet performed in front of a live audience.

BLACKPINK
"How You Like That"
minji

Once quarantine policies were lifted, groups were eager to greet their fans around the world. All of a sudden, it felt like there was a K-pop concert to attend every other month – perhaps because I was now following multiple groups, but also because there was a true boom in US and world tours: In 2018, there were 93 US concerts by 15 unique K-pop acts. In 2022, there were 420 shows by 48 K-pop acts.

Growing up, I couldn’t imagine attending a K-pop concert in the States - now, I’m one seat in a stadium of diverse fans screaming perfect Korean lyrics.

(2023–Today)

Late 20s and Late-Stage K-Pop

eunice

A few years ago, I visited Minji in New York and we were able to reconnect again after the pandemic. While catching up, we naturally asked each other how we were keeping up with K-pop. We reminisced about the "2nd generation" when we tenderly remembered K-pop’s deep impact on us. We realized a lot had changed since we were dancing to BoA in her parents’ living room. The success of global groups like KATSEYE and XG are showing that the K-pop model may work without the “K” and in multiple markets. Most recently, KPop Demon Hunters became the most-watched film in Netflix history and earned K-pop its first-ever Grammy Award.

HUNTR/X
"Golden"
minji

For many veteran K-pop fans, the speed and breadth of the industry’s growth comes with mixed feelings. In a survey we put out to fans in 2025, many respondents grieved the loss of the stronger identities of prior generations.

We can’t help but relate to the responses – though we enjoy the trendy sounds and complex choreo of 5th gen groups, our connection to the genre has become more intellectual than personal. As we get older and the new releases blend in with Western tracks on our playlists, we find ourselves seeking comfort in the familiar sounds of BTS, KARA, 2NE1.

ILLIT
"Magnetic"
minji

At the same time, we have the new excitement of seeing one of our oldest passions become so well-known and well-loved by new generations. We pass students filming K-Pop covers in Times Square and Golden Gate Park. We hear coworkers’ children playing KPop Demon Hunters in the background on calls. We see it passing the stage of sensationalism, becoming a given on the charts.

eunice

Maybe this is simply what happens when you grow older alongside a culture you once had to explain. It becomes bigger, glossier, more celebrated - something you once had to defend is now the thing everyone wants to understand. The music we discovered on YouTube now has hundreds of millions of views, and the groups we followed now are beloved by fans all around the world.

Maybe the real legacy isn’t in the chart numbers or streaming views but in what it gave us: the chance to see our language, culture, and passions reflected on TV screens and concert halls. Maybe it’s in witnessing something you loved become something the world loves too, without losing what made it special to you in the first place.

We were there when it was for us, and we are here as it finds the world.
