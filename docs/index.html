Early Childhood

eunice
Minji and I first met at a Korean language school in the Michigan suburbs. It was the early 2000s, and every Saturday morning our parents, drawn to Detroit by the promise of the auto industry, dropped us off to learn Korean language and culture. We were the same age, and in a classroom full of kids wearing sparkly Limited Too tops or oversized Pokmon and Dragon Ball Z T-shirts, we found each other through K-pop.
eunice
That shared obsession quickly turned into regular playdates at her house, where wed sit cross-legged in the living room and play BoAs No. 1 on a silver CD player. Later, when YouTube arrived, wed crowd around her family desktop and watch the music videos, our eyes glued to the screen. We watched her on repeat studying the way she moved, the precision of her choreography, and her cathartic vocals. Wed rewind the same eight-count over and over, trying to get it right. Sometimes we did. Mostly, we laughed, got it wrong, and hit play again.
BoA
"No. 1"
Seo Taiji & Boys
" (I Know)"
minji
Before leaving Korea for the States, my parents burnt CDs with their favorite Korean songs to bring a part of home with them. We would all try to sing and rap along to Seo Taijis (I Know) in the car, finding comfort in Korean words blasting in our ears as we drove through the Michigan suburbs.
eunice
Modern K-pop is widely traced back to the 1990s, when Seo Taiji and Boys regulars at Moon Night, a club frequented by Black American GIs in Seoul learned and took hip hop and New Jack Swing to Korean audiences. Their sound shook the music scene and sparked a cultural shift. In the years that followed, as the country recovered from years of political and financial crisis, the Korean government began to see pop culture - including music, television, and film - as a strategic export and form of soft power.
eunice
But to us, it was much more personal - a way to make sense of who we were, even if we didnt know thats what we were doing.
eunice
Our childhood drummed to this steady beat of K-pop together until 2006, when Minjis family left for California and I moved to Korea the following year.
(20062012)

Growing Up Our First Fandom

Super Junior
"U"
minji
Adjusting to a new city and school was tough. I was suddenly surrounded by Korean classmates - a huge shift from the Michigan suburbs that came with a different set of cultural expectations and norms. At recess, my classmates would talk about a K-pop group called Super Junior. At first, I only acted like I knew and liked SuJu to fit in. But shortly after, my friend showed me a music video Super Juniors newest title track, U.
minji
I still remember how my ears perked up with the strum of the guitar intro, how my head bopped involuntarily as the 11 members popped and locked to the beat drop. I ran home and immediately googled how to torrent music so that I could add it to my mp3 player. I couldnt wait to go to school the next day to talk about the song and show off the dance moves I picked up overnight. I was officially a fan.
eunice
I had also discovered Super Junior after a visit to Korea that summer. I remember buying the CD, Twins, and inside was a fold-out sheet of each member serving face, their names printed underneath. You were supposed to pick one. We didnt call it bias back then, the vocabulary hadnt reached us yet. It was just understood that you needed a favorite. For several years, mine was Kibum.
BIGBANG
"Lies"
minji
By this point, Eunice and I had become pen pals, emailing back and forth about anything and everything. When we realized we were both E.L.Fs (Ever Lasting Fans, the official Super Junior fandom), we freaked out. But as the years went by, our tastes started to change. By middle school, we had moved on to hot new groups like Girls Generation, Wondergirls, and BIGBANG.
SHINee
"Replay"
minji
These legendary acts were capturing the hearts of the Korean general public and taking the Hallyu Wave all across Asia and into immigrant communities around the world. After my family moved back to Michigan, my high schools Asian Pacific American Club decided to incorporate K-pop into our yearly cultural show performance. I couldnt believe that I was learning the choreography for SHINees Replay with my non-Korean friends, performing in front of an auditorium full of non-Korean classmates. While most of my classmates couldnt point to Korea on a map, my small but tight-knit community of Asian immigrant kids were streaming K-pop videos on YouTube together after school.
minji
We were living through the Golden Age of K-Pop from opposite sides of the world one in the homeland, the other in the diaspora. Though we were too young to understand the forces driving this wave, the soft power that South Korea was amassing via these cultural exports, we were authentic participants of the buzz and excitement.
eunice
In 2012, Psys Gangnam Style became the first YouTube video to hit one billion videos. At its peak, Gangnam Style was generating over 11 million views per day, and YouTube even had to upgrade its view counter system, which had maxed out at 2,147,483,647 views a limit based on 32-bit integer coding. Suddenly, Psy was everywhere and, for many people around the world, it was their first exposure to K-pop.
eunice
I remember watching Psy teach the Gangnam Style dance to Brittany Spears on Ellen and getting chills. I didnt even love the songbut there was something thrilling about watching the world finally catch up to what we already knew. Korean pop wasnt just foreign. It was fun, silly, catchyand unmissable.
(20122017)

College Years The K-Pop Boom

eunice
Until around 2012, K-pop was growing at a rate that was manageable for an early fan like myself. In its formative years from 1992 to 2002, often labeled as the first generation of K-pop, only 33 groups debuted in total.

Young Adulthood & The Pandemic

Super Junior
"U"
minji
After graduating college, I moved to New York, where I was once again in a big new city away from home but with more access to Korean culture. I spent many weekends at Koreatown pochas, where they would play the newest K-pop music videos on the TVs. When 2 members of the girl group (G)I-DLE (now i-dle) participated in the virtual group K/DA for the game League of Legends, and SM powerhouse group Aespa debuted with an AI and multiverse concept, I knew we had entered a new era. While these innovations felt foreign at first, I was intrigued by the new soundscapes and fresh performances these 4th gen groups were bringing to the scene.
